In her novel *The Fountainhead*, first published in 1943, Ayn Rand explores the concept of creative purity as a fundamental component of man's self-expression, and hence freedom. The concept is developed in the main character, the architect Howard Roarke, and his struggle against American conventional wisdom in his chosen profession. Roark insists on setting his own standards and being beholden to no one as pillars of his creative process. When Steve Jobs returned to Apple in 1997, he reorganized the firm, exerting, with Jony Ive, undisputed control over the design process, which was similarly shielded from any outside interference, driving Apple's innovative products and underlying the firm's rebirth.

In the movie *Ford v. Ferrari* (2019), British sports car engineer and racing enthusiast Ken Miles lists as one of his conditions; to take on the assignment of helping Ford beat Ferrari in the 24 Hours of Le Mans via the finetuning of a superfast sports car, the GT40 Mk I prototype; that there be no interference from the suits at Ford Motors - that he be given full creative license. With some difficulty, Caroll Shelby is able to provide him with this freedom, and this ensures Ford's victory in the 24 Hours in 1966. Miles dies in 1966, but his legacy takes Ford to victories in the race again in 1967, 1968, and 1969.

Where did Jobs' passion for developing awesome products come from? He is said to have wanted to "make a dent in the universe." This may be tied to the fact that his biological parents abandoned him, putting him up for adoption. Was his superego concerned with leaving a legacy behind after his demise? Towards the end of his life, he wondered to Walter Isaacson, his biographer, whether Bill Gates had really wanted to become obscenely rich when he set out to build Microsoft, or whether he failed at making great products, selling his principles instead. Was Bill Gates so different from him on the plagiarism front though - didn't Steve "borrow" the Macintosh GUI from Xerox PARC as well?

Rand dedicates a good portion of *The Fountainhead* to hashing out her concept of creative purity. For her, the human spirit is the great life-giving force of the universe; the creator and conqueror of the ideal. Rand believes the creator is, at bottom, selfish. Instead of being prompted by a desire to serve others, he is prompted by a desire to manifest his truth. Those who live off the creations of others tend to shun creators if their discoveries will deprive them of their jobs. A good example of this would be AI taking over tasks still carried out by humans in exchange for payment. The end goal of the creator is thus his creation and not its users. And so the creator holds his truth above all things and against all men.

In 1971, Jobs (who was then 16, one year away from entering college) and Wozniak (who was then 21) had their first taste of an entrepreneurial venture together when they began selling Wozniak's Blue Boxes. Jobs showed then he had a flair for pricing and selling products. Later, when Jobs and Wozniak founded Apple in 1976, Jobs still wasn't creating anything. The initial big seller, the 1977 Apple II, was almost entirely Wozniak's brainchild. True, Jobs had shown a talent for packaging things nicely - the external design elements of the Apple II can be ascribed to him. Additionally, he had begun to show he had a knack for unveiling products. He popped his cherry on this front when the Apple II was unveiled at the West Coast Computer Faire in San Francisco in 1977.

Due to his electric charisma, Jobs was great at "rallying the troops." From his college pal Robert Friedland he had picked up the habit of staring unblinkingly at people. He knew how to enthuse potential collaborators with a drive to create something new and excellent. Jobs said that dropping LSD and adopting a Zen lifestyle helped him see the other side of the coin, reinforcing his sense that creating great things was more important than making money, "putting things back into the stream of history and human consciousness." The seven months he spent soul searching in Indian villages when he was 19 years old also taught him valuable life lessons. In particular, he saw first-hand the power of intuition against purely rational thought. He then spent the remainder of his life practicing calming his rational mind in order to be present in the moment and so see and hear things more clearly.

Similarly, Roarke's modernist architectural leanings stem from his intuition rather than his intellect. He is constantly fighting the urge the profession has in the novel to rehash any number of architectural conventions taken from ancient buildings that he sees as out of place in his own time. Roark can often be seen at one with nature, either about to plunge from a cliff into a body of water, or absorbing his natural surroundings. In the city, he is customarily bewitched watching the skyscrapers above his head. He does not accept to compromise with his principles concerning style and the unity of the buildings he designs. Consequently, he loses plenty of profitable contracts and ultimately blasts one of his greatest creations, the Cortlandt housing estate. Jobs was also known to stay true to his design principles, even when his engineers told him they could lead to technical failures.
